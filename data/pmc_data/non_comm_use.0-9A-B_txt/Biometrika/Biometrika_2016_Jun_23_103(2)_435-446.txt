Quantile classifiers are distance-based classifiers that require a single parameter, regardless of the dimension, and classify observations according to a sum of weighted componentwise distances of the components of an observation to the within-class quantiles. An optimal percentage for the quantiles can be chosen by minimizing the misclassification error in the training sample. It is shown that this choice is consistent for the classification rule with the asymptotically optimal quantile and that under some assumptions, as the number of variables goes to infinity, the probability of correct classification converges to unity. The effect of skewness of the distributions of the predictor variables is discussed. The optimal quantile classifier gives low misclassification rates in a comprehensive simulation study and in a real-data application.

1. Introduction
Supervised classification has received a great deal of attention. Classification methods can be broadly divided into parametric methods, which make distributional assumptions (e.g., Bensmail & Celeux, 1996; Hastie & Tibishirani, 1996; Hand & Yu, 2001; Fraley & Raftery, 2002), and nonparametric methods, which concentrate on the local vicinity of the point to be classified (e.g., Cover & Hart, 1967; Mika et al., 1999). Implementing classification methods in high dimensions can be computationally demanding, because of the curse of dimensionality (Bellman, 1961). One way to address this problem is to rely on portions of the conditional distribution of the features given the class labels. Distance-based classifiers use the partial information of the class-conditional distributions; centroid-based methods have been successfully applied to gene expression data (Dudoit et al., 2002; Tibshirani et al., 2002; Dabney, 2005; Fan & Fan, 2008), and median-based classifiers (Jörnsten, 2004; Ghosh & Chaudhuri, 2005) represent a more robust alternative for distributions with heavy tails. 4. Some issues and extensions
4.1. Standardization
Like other classifiers, the quantile classifier depends on the scaling of the variables. This dependence can be removed by standardizing the variables. Standardization can be seen as implicit reweighting of the variables. Optimally, variables are treated in such a way that their relative weights reflect their relative information content for classification.

In practice, standardization may be inadvisable when variables have the same measurement units and there are reasons to expect that the information content of the variables for classification may be indicated by their variation. This will play a role in the example in §5.2. Crossvalidation could also help to decide whether standardization would be beneficial.

Where variables are standardized, transformation to unit pooled within-class variance, as estimated from the training data, can be expected to improve classification performance compared with the overall variance. This is because the separation between classes may contribute strongly to the overall variance, so variables with a strong separation between classes and hence a large amount of classification information will be implicitly downweighted, whereas standardization to unit pooled within-class variance will downweight variables for which the classes are heterogeneous and which are therefore not so useful for classification.

4.4. Dependence
Distance-based classifiers defined according to (1), including the quantile classifier, aggregate information from the variables without taking dependence between them into account. The simulations in §5.1 show that good classification results can be obtained in the case of dependence. Distance-based classifiers do not require independence, only that the information which separates the classes can be picked up from the original variables. Using marginal information from all variables is a way, other than dimension reduction, to avoid overfitting with high-dimensional data. This strategy is superior to dimension-reduction approaches in some situations but inferior in others; no classifier can be expected to be universally optimal. In situations where the classification information is poorly represented in the original variables, one could apply the quantile classifier to principal components or independent components (Hyvärinen et al., 2001). Preliminary simulations show that using principal components may lead to improved results with approximately normal distributions, but may give unstable results for high-dimensional data with skew or heavy-tailed marginal distributions.

5.2. Real-data example
For illustration, we apply the quantile classifier to data collected while testing a new method to detect bioaerosol particles based on gaseous plasma electrochemistry. The presence of such particles in air has a big impact on health, but monitoring bioaerosols poses great technical challenges. Sarantaridis et al. (2012) attempted to distinguish several different bioaerosols based on voltage changes over time on eight different electrodes when particles passed a premixed laminar hydrogen-oxygen-nitrogen flame. The resulting data consist of eight time series with 301 observations for each particle. Sarantaridis et al. (2012) discussed how the relevant information in each time series can be summarized in terms of six characteristic features, namely maximum voltage in series, minimum voltage in series, maximum voltage change caused by the electrode, difference between final and initial voltage, length of positive change caused by the electrode, and length of negative change caused by the electrode. A seventh variable used in Sarantaridis et al. (2012) is omitted here based on the chemists' recommendation. We are therefore left with 48 variables.

We apply a variable standardization scheme based on subject knowledge, which is motivated by the expectation of the chemists that the size of variation in voltage and length of effect is informative and that electrodes and variables for which the electrode causes stronger variation are actually more important for discrimination.
