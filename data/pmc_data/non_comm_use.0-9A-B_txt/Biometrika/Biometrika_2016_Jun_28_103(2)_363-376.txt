Although skewness in longitudinal data may be reduced by applying a suitable transformation, difficulties may arise in interpreting the results on the data's original scale. More problematically, different transformations may be needed at different time-points, as with the cattle growth data. Therefore it may be preferable to model the untransformed data using a family of skewed distributions. However, nonnormal multivariate distributions do not necessarily accord well with antedependence. For example, Baba et al. (2004) showed that within the class of elliptical distributions, a subset of the variables is conditionally independent of the rest if and only if the variables' joint distribution is multivariate normal with corresponding zero partial correlations. It can be shown by a similar argument that any subclass of skew-elliptical distributions that is consistent with antedependence must include the multivariate skew-normal distribution. In this article we extend the unstructured normal antedependence model to skew-normal antedependence models of two types. The first is obtained by imposing antedependence on the multivariate skew-normal distribution, while the second is constructed as a sequential autoregressive process with skew-normal innovations. 7. Discussion
The foregoing development has not allowed for missing data. For data that follow an ad(\documentclass[12pt]{minimal}
\usepackage{amsmath}
\usepackage{wasysym} 
\usepackage{amsfonts} 
\usepackage{amssymb} 
\usepackage{amsbsy}
\usepackage{upgreek}
\usepackage{mathrsfs}
\setlength{\oddsidemargin}{-69pt}
\begin{document}
}{}$p$\end{document}) multivariate normal model, the expectation-maximization algorithm is a simple and effective way of obtaining maximum likelihood estimates when some data are ignorably missing; see Zimmerman & Núñez-Antón (2010, pp. 151–4). Unfortunately, this algorithm is much more difficult to implement for ad(\documentclass[12pt]{minimal}
\usepackage{amsmath}
\usepackage{wasysym} 
\usepackage{amsfonts} 
\usepackage{amssymb} 
\usepackage{amsbsy}
\usepackage{upgreek}
\usepackage{mathrsfs}
\setlength{\oddsidemargin}{-69pt}
\begin{document}
}{}$p$\end{document}) multivariate skew-normal and ad(\documentclass[12pt]{minimal}
\usepackage{amsmath}
\usepackage{wasysym} 
\usepackage{amsfonts} 
\usepackage{amssymb} 
\usepackage{amsbsy}
\usepackage{upgreek}
\usepackage{mathrsfs}
\setlength{\oddsidemargin}{-69pt}
\begin{document}
}{}$p$\end{document}) autoregressive skew-normal models, because neither comprises an exponential family. Furthermore, the algorithm's performance in conjunction with penalized, rather than ordinary, maximum likelihood estimation is unknown. Consequently, for data that follow either of the ad(\documentclass[12pt]{minimal}
\usepackage{amsmath}
\usepackage{wasysym} 
\usepackage{amsfonts} 
\usepackage{amssymb} 
\usepackage{amsbsy}
\usepackage{upgreek}
\usepackage{mathrsfs}
\setlength{\oddsidemargin}{-69pt}
\begin{document}
}{}$p$\end{document}) models presented here, we recommend using multiple imputation (Rubin, 1987) to deal with missingness.

Although the two models presented herein are the most direct extensions of normal antedependence models possible, additional models could result from combining antedependence with skew-normality in other ways. One possibility worthy of future investigation is a Gaussian copula regression model (Pitt et al., 2006; Song et al., 2009) with banded-inverse covariance structure and skew-normal marginals. Antedependence models that can accommodate more extreme skewness than is possible under skew-normality also warrant development.

Supplementary material available at Biometrika online includes an illustration of some of the behaviours possible for the marginal skewnesses of an ad(1) multivariate skew-normal model, as well as additional details of the estimation procedures, simulation results and data analysis.
