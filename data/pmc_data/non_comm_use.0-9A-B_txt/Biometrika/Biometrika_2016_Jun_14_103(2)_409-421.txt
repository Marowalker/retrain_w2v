1. Introduction
In recent decades, structural changes have been observed in datasets collected from various areas of study, such as genetics (Fearnhead & Liu, 2007), speech signals (Davis et al., 2008) and environmental statistics (Lu et al., 2010). Both the frequentist approach, which assumes that changepoints are unknown parameters, and the Bayesian approach, which assumes that the changepoints follow prior distributions, are considered in the literature (Fearnhead, 2005, 2006; Erdman & Emerson, 2008). Although the detection of changepoints in independent data has been extensively studied, there has been relatively little discussion on changepoint analysis in multivariate time series. Recently, nonparametric statistical methods for changepoint detection in multivariate time series have been proposed (Aue et al., 2009; Matteson & James, 2014; Preuß et al., 2015). Aue et al. (2009) propose a nonparametric approach for testing changes in the covariance structure of multivariate time series. If a parametric model is appropriate, then changepoint detection can be conducted using penalized likelihood functions (Davis et al., 2008; Lu et al., 2010). Although the use of likelihood can achieve high statistical efficiency, in many complicated statistical models, the likelihood may involve high-dimensional inverse covariance matrices or integrals, which are computationally infeasible.

To overcome limitations in evaluating the full likelihood, Lindsay (1988) proposes the use of composite likelihood, which combines the likelihoods for subsets of the data. For example, the pairwise likelihood considers the product of the bivariate densities of all possible pairs of observations. When computational constraints such as high-dimensional integrals or inverting large covariance matrices are involved, composite likelihood may be computed more efficiently than the full likelihood, while statistical efficiency may be retained; see Lindsay (1988), Varin & Vidoni (2005), Varin (2008) and Varin et al. (2011). Due to its simplicity and attractive asymptotic properties, composite likelihood has been widely used, for example in image analysis (Nott & Rydén, 1999), spatial generalized linear mixed models (Varin et al., 2005), longitudinal time series (Molenberghs & Verbeke, 2005) and genetics (Larribe & Fearnhead, 2011).

In this paper, we derive a criterion for changepoint estimation based on the minimum description length principle. With the use of pairwise likelihood, changepoint estimation enjoys robustness and high statistical efficiency, and thus can be applied in many situations. We show that the number and locations of changepoints can be consistently estimated under mild assumptions, even if the model is misspecified.

Computational feasibility is another challenge, since changepoint estimation usually involves optimization over all possible configurations of changepoints. Some popular optimization methods, including binary segmentation (Zhang et al., 2010) and stochastic search algorithms such as the genetic algorithm (Davis et al., 2006), are fast but only provide approximately optimal solutions. On the other hand, dynamic programming algorithms (Killick et al., 2012; K. Haynes, I. A. Eckley and P. Fearnhead, arXiv: 1412.3617) provide exact solutions. In particular, the pruned exact linear time method (Killick et al., 2012) reduces the computational cost substantially by pruning away some irrelevant computational steps. In this paper, we apply it to yield an exact and computationally efficient solution to the optimization problem in changepoint estimation.

4. Implementation
4.1. Pruned exact linear time method
In many changepoint problems (Davis et al., 2006, 2008; Lu et al., 2010), finding the best segmentation involves a computationally intensive search. In general, search algorithms can be classified as stochastic or deterministic. The former include genetic algorithms (Davis et al., 2006) and may involve large scale simulation. Although the computation time can be controlled, a number of tuning parameters are involved and solution optimality is not guaranteed. Among deterministic methods, binary segmentation (Zhang et al., 2010) is fast but yields only an approximate solution. Exact methods such as optimal partitioning (Jackson et al., 2005), which performs an exhaustive search, remains computationally prohibitive when the sample is large. Killick et al. (2012) recently proposed an exact algorithm that achieves linear computational cost when the number of changepoints increases with sample size. We adopt their algorithm in this paper.
