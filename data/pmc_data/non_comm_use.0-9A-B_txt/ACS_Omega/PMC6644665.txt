Unsymmetrical B(MIDA)-containing
diborons of potential synthetic
utility were found to be synthesized from the readily available (neop)B–B(neop)
and (HO)2B–B(OH)2, and the procedure
was extended to the generation of symmetrical (MIDA)B–B(MIDA).
NMR and X-ray crystal structure studies revealed that B(MIDA) units
of all of the diborons obtained in this study were in rigid tetrahedral
environment.

Introduction
Compounds having a
boron–boron σ-bond, diborons (alternatively
termed diboron(4) and diborane(4)),1 have
proven to be essential reagents for synthesizing diverse organoboron
compounds, which play a pivotal role in modern synthetic organic chemistry,2 through Miyaura–Ishiyama borylation (C–X
borylation, X: halogen, OTf, etc.) (eq 1, Scheme 1),3 Hartwig–Miyaura
C–H borylation (eq 2),4 diborylation
of unsaturated C–C bonds (eq 3),5 and so on.6

Scheme 1 Synthesis of Organoboron
Compounds with Diborons
Among various diborons prepared thus far, bench-stable
and easy-to-handle
tetraalkoxydiborons such as bis(pinacolato)diboron [(pin)B–B(pin)]7 and bis(neopentyl glycolato)diboron [(neop)B–B(neop)]8 have been the mainstay in the above borylation
reactions (Figure 1), and recent attention has also been directed toward the use of
unsymmetrical diborons1a,9 aiming at the installation of
different boryl groups into the organic frameworks and opposite regioselectivities
to those with (pin)B–B(pin), thus leading to an enlarged structural
diversity of organoboron compounds to be accessed.

Figure 1 Representative diborons
employed in various borylation reactions.

In particular, we have been engaged on synthetic utilization
of
(pin)B–B(dan)10 (dan: 1,8-diaminonaphthalene)
for copper-catalyzed B(dan)-installing reactions including hydroboration,11a,11b borylstannylation,11c carboboration,11d and substitutional borylation11e (Scheme 2), where their unique site- and regioselectivities are mainly governed
by the diminished Lewis acidity of the B(dan) moiety.12 In this context, our efforts have been continuously devoted
to synthesizing new diborons having a boron moiety of diminished Lewis
acidity, and we report herein on the first synthesis of unsymmetrical
diborons containing a B(MIDA) unit (MIDA: N-methyliminodiacetic
acid) (Figure 2),13 whose Lewis acidity is substantially reduced
by tetrahedralization of a boron center via intramolecular coordination
with an amine moiety, and on their structural properties based upon
the NMR and X-ray crystal structure analysis. Further application
to the synthesis of a sp3–sp3 diboron,14 (MIDA)B–B(MIDA), is also documented.

Results and Discussion
We first conducted the reaction of (neop)B–B(neop) with
MIDA in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) at 80 °C under microwave irradiation
conditions (entry 1 in Table 1) and observed that one of the neop moieties on the boron
atom was smoothly substituted by MIDA to give an unsymmetrical diboron,
(neop)B–B(MIDA) (1a), in 55% yield. The use of
DMSO as a solvent was found to be crucial for the formation of 1a, and thus the reaction in other solvents was totally unsuccessful
(entries 2–9). Microwave irradiation was not always required
for synthesizing 1a, and conventional heating in DMSO
under slightly modified conditions increased the yield up to 74% (entry
10).

In marked contrast

substitution of a B(pin) moiety of (pin)B–B(pin) by MIDA turned
out to be impracticable even under harsh conditions (DMSO, 250 °C,

was produced (Scheme 3). On the other hand, treatment of (HO)2B–B(OH)2 with MIDA and pinacol in DMSO resulted in the facile formation
of 1b in 58% yield (Scheme 4).

While preparing B(MIDA)-containing
unsymmetrical diborons 1a and 1b, we became
aware of the generation
of (MIDA)B–B(MIDA) (1c) as a byproduct (Scheme 5). As described for
entry 1 in Table 2,
the symmetrical diboron was accessible in 50% yield by treating (neop)B–B(neop)
with an excess of MIDA in DMSO at 120 °C, whereas a similar reaction
under milder conditions (3 equiv of MIDA, 100 °C) scarcely gave 1c (entry 2). It should be noted that the addition of water
significantly promoted the reaction, affording 1c in
58 or 57% yield under otherwise identical conditions (entries 3 and
4). In addition, an 86% yield of 1c was also accessible
by treating (HO)2B–B(OH)2 with MIDA (3
equiv) in the absence of water (Scheme 6), which implies that the presence of water may facilitate
the hydrolysis of (neop)B–B(neop) and/or (neop)B–B(MIDA)
intermediate (1a) during the reaction process, resulting
in the efficient formation of 1c (entries 3 and 4 in Table 3).

1H NMR spectra of 1 showed the AX-coupling
pattern for the methylene protons of the B(MIDA) moiety in each case,
which denotes a tetrahedral environment of the boron center arising
from intramolecular N–B coordination (Figure 3).15 In addition,
the B(MIDA) moieties of 1c were found to be magnetically
equivalent (Figure 3c), which indicates a symmetrical sp3–sp3 bonding between two boron atoms. The bicyclic structures of the
B(MIDA) proved to be rigid, as the methylene protons did not coalesce
at all upon heating (up to 70 °C) in variable-temperature NMR
experiments. Interestingly, a little shift in the methylene protons
(upfield) and the methyl protons (on nitrogen, downfield) was observed
at the higher temperature in all of the cases.16 Furthermore, the sp3 hybridization in the B(MIDA)
was also confirmed by 11B NMR, where their δ values
lie in the range of 10–11 ppm, being similar to those of related
diboron9a and B(MIDA) boronates.15,17

The molecular structures
of diborons (1a and 1c) were also determined
by X-ray crystal structure analysis,
which reveals a pyramidalized environment around the B(MIDA) moieties
(Figures 4 and 5). The N–B coordination
is evident from their bond length (1.660 Å on average) (Table 3), which is similar
to those of R–B(MIDA) compounds16,17 and picoline-coordinated
(cat)B–B(cat),18 and the B(sp3) centers deriving therefrom have proven to be somewhat distorted
from the ideal sp3 hybridization (tetrahedral character:
62.8% (1a) and 62.4% (1c)).19 In addition, the B–B bond in these diborons slightly
elongates (1.721 Å on average) as compared with that in (pin)B–B(pin)
(B(sp2)–B(sp2): 1.710 Å)9b,20 owing to the boron tetrahedralization.

Conclusions
We have demonstrated
that unsymmetrical B(MIDA)-containing diborons
[(neop)B–B(MIDA) and (pin)B–B(MIDA)] can facilely be
synthesized by simply substituting (neop)B–B(neop) or (HO)2B–B(OH)2 of high availability with MIDA,
and that a modified method leads to the efficient generation of (MIDA)B–B(MIDA)
having a B(sp3)–B(sp3) bond. The NMR
and X-ray crystal structure studies revealed that all of the B(MIDA)
moieties were in a tetrahedral environment, and these sp3-hybridized boron structures were found to be rigid even upon heating.
Moreover, the B–B bonds are elongated by introducing the B(MIDA)
unit as compared with those of B(sp2)–B(sp2) diborons such as (pin)B–B(pin) and (cat)B–B(cat),21 which would result from the increased p-character
in the B–B bonding. Further studies on the synthetic application
of the diborons to various borylation reactions as well as on the
synthesis of other B(MIDA)-containing diborons are in progress.

General Information
All of the manipulations of oxygen-
and moisture-sensitive materials were conducted with a standard Schlenk
technique under an argon atmosphere. Nuclear magnetic resonance spectra
were recorded by a Varian System 500 (1H, 500 MHz; 13C, 125 MHz; 11B, 160 MHz) or Varian 400MR (1H, 400 MHz) spectrometer using a residual proton in DMSO-d6 (1H, δ = 2.49) or DMSO-d6 (13C, δ = 39.5) as an internal
standard and boron trifluoride diethyl etherate (11B, δ
= 0.00) as an external standard. 1H NMR data are reported
as follows: chemical shift, multiplicity (s = singlet, d = doublet),
coupling constants (Hz), integration. High-resolution mass spectra
were obtained with a Thermo Fisher Scientific LTQ Orbitrap XL spectrometer.
Melting points were measured with Yanaco Micro Melting Point apparatus
and uncorrected. All of the microwave reactions (Biotage, Initiator+)
were conducted in a sealed tube, and the reaction temperature was
maintained by an external infrared sensor. Unless otherwise noted,
commercially available reagents were used without purification. DMSO
was dried over activated molecular sieves 3A.

Synthesis of 1a
A DMSO solution (10 mL)
of (neop)B–B(neop) (1.13 g, 5.00 mmol) and MIDA (1.10 g, 7.50
mmol) was stirred at 80 °C for 6 h before the removal of DMSO
by bulb-to-bulb distillation (3.00–5.00 mmHg, 90 °C).
The residue was extracted three times with acetone (70 mL), and an
insoluble solid was removed by filtration. Evaporation of the solvent
followed by washing with toluene (60 mL) gave 1a (0.99
g, 74%) as a colorless solid.
